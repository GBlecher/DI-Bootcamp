Biography


Desmond Doss was a U.S. Army medic during World War II and is best known for his remarkable acts of heroism during the Battle of Okinawa, for which he became the first conscientious objector to receive the Medal of Honor. Born on February 7, 1919, in Virginia, Doss was raised in a devout Seventh-day Adventist family, which instilled in him a strong commitment to pacifism.

Despite his beliefs, Doss felt a duty to serve his country and enlisted in the Army in 1942. He chose to become a medic, refusing to carry a weapon due to his religious convictions. His determination and bravery were evident during the intense combat of Okinawa, where he single-handedly rescued dozens of wounded soldiers while under enemy fire, demonstrating extraordinary courage without ever firing a shot.

For his actions, which included saving an estimated 75 men during the brutal fighting on Hacksaw Ridge, Doss was awarded the Medal of Honor by President Harry S. Truman in 1945. Doss's story has inspired numerous books and films, notably the 2016 movie "Hacksaw Ridge," which brought renewed attention to his legacy.

After the war, Doss returned to civilian life, marrying Frances Doss and having one son. He lived a quiet life and continued to promote his faith until his death on March 23, 2006, in Chattanooga, Tennessee. Desmond Doss is celebrated not only for his bravery in the face of combat but also for his steadfast adherence to his beliefs.
